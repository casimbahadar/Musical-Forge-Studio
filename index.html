📖 New here? How to forge a theme

Quick start: tap Preview to hear the current recipe → tap 🎲 New melody until you hear a tune you like → tweak the mood controls → Save.

The golden rule: a theme is a recipe, and the Seed is its DNA. The same recipe + the same seed produces the identical music, every time, forever. The 🎲 button changes only the seed — a brand-new melody in the same style.

What each control does:

Instrument — who plays the melody. Piano and e-piano are versatile; harp/bell/marimba feel gentle or magical; brass/saw lead/organ feel powerful; chip square is retro; strings are cinematic; flute is airy.

Key — how high or low the whole piece sits. Mostly flavor; when unsure, leave it.

Mode / feel — the emotional color, and the strongest single control: Major = bright & happy · Minor = serious or sad · Harmonic minor = dramatic, villain energy · Phrygian = menacing · Lydian = wonder & magic · Dorian = earthy adventure · Mixolydian = playful.

Progression — the chord loop underneath the melody; the names describe the arc ("Heroic" rises, "Creeping threat" tightens, "Calm" drifts).

Tempo — speed in beats per minute. Rough map: 60–95 calm places · 96–125 travel & towns · 126–160 battles and chases.

Bass — the low end's personality: Whole notes = still and calm · Walk = gentle strolling · Pulse = bouncy · Octave drive = relentless action.

Drums — energy floor: None/Sparse = ambient · Light = strolling · Drive = action · Double-time = boss fight. Drive and Double-time also add a battle intro: a drum fill and a rising lead run before the theme kicks in.

Melody density — how busy the tune is: Sparse = long singing notes · Busy = rapid and urgent.

Length — 12 bars suits areas and battles; 8 bars suits menus and shorter loops.

Arpeggio layer — a fast background sparkle that adds urgency; great for battles and storms, too restless for lullabies.

Recipes to steal: Boss battle = harmonic minor + Creeping threat + 145 BPM + Double-time + Octave drive + Busy + arpeggio. Peaceful village = major + Calm + 90 + Light + Walk + marimba. Mystery cave = phrygian + Dark pulse + 72 + Sparse + Whole notes + organ.

Everything else on this page:

The player at the top shows what's selected. Play loops the current theme (tap again to stop), Export WAV renders it to a real audio file you can keep and use anywhere, and the four bars are level meters — LEAD is the melody, PAD the background chords, BASS the low end, DRUMS the rhythm. Watching them helps you hear which layer is which.

Examples are ready-made recipes: tap one and it loads into the forge and plays, so you can learn by remixing — change one control at a time and hear what it does.

Your themes (appears after your first Save): tap a card to play it. Each card has ♫ WAV (export that theme as audio), ⬇ JSON (download its tiny recipe file), and ✕ (delete). Saves are stored on this device.

Save vs Copy recipe vs ⬇ JSON: Save keeps a theme in your list here. Copy recipe and ⬇ JSON both give you the recipe as text/file — for backup, or to share: anyone who Imports your recipe hears exactly the music you heard, because recipes are deterministic.

⬇ themes.json (all) downloads every theme (stock + yours) as one file. Import JSON reads recipe files back in — it accepts theme recipes, Remix-Lab FX presets, and Score Composer scores, even mixed in one file, and sorts each to the right place.

↗ Share on any saved card posts it to the 🌐 Community board — a global gallery everyone using the app can browse, play and download. Sharing needs a creator name (kept for next time, shown beside your posts), and a device-held key means only you can post or delete under that name — no account, no sign-in. Open 🌐 Community board from the home screen to browse everyone's creations, filter by type and sort.

The Remix Lab and Score Composer pages have their own guides just like this one — look for the 🎚 and 🎼 panels there.

Remix Lab

Remix Lab

Two ways to load audio: attach a file from your device, or paste a direct link to an audio file (a URL ending in .mp3, .ogg or .wav).

✔ Works: direct file links from sites that allow cross-site access — OpenGameArt and Pixabay downloads, GitHub raw links, your own hosting.
✘ Doesn't work: YouTube, Spotify or SoundCloud links, converter sites (ytmp3 and similar), or links to web pages rather than files. Streaming platforms block downloads by design and their terms prohibit ripping — that applies even to copyright-free videos. If a direct link fails, the site blocks cross-site fetching: download the file and attach it instead — that always works.

Use audio you have the rights to. Works best on music without vocals.
🎚 First time? What each slider does

Start simple: pick a preset, press Play, then drag one slider at a time while it plays — you'll hear exactly what each one does. Reset FX takes you back to neutral.

Speed & pitch — left = slower and deeper, right = faster and higher. They move together, like a record player.

Drive / distortion — low = warmth, high = crunchy and aggressive.

Bit crush — retro digital grit; the readout shows how few "steps" the sound is squeezed into. Higher = more broken and 8-bit.

Low cut — removes bass. Push it up for a thin, small sound. High cut — removes treble. Pull it down for muffled or underwater. Cut both toward the middle = old radio.

Brightness — adds or removes sparkle on top.

Reverb — the size of the room, from none to cathedral.

Echo — amount is how loud the repeats are, time is the gap between them, repeats is how long they trail off. High repeats = dub-style tails.

Tape wobble — the pitch wavers like a worn cassette.

Loop keeps the track going while you tweak; Reverse plays it backwards (this one restarts playback).

Found a sound you love? Name it and Save FX preset below — it'll be waiting next time. Export WAV bakes your exact settings into an audio file.

Everything else on this page:

After loading a track you'll see its length plus an estimated key and tempo. Estimates are usually right on instrumental music but can miss on drum-heavy or free-tempo tracks — trust your ears.

Send key+tempo to Forge jumps to the Theme Forge page with the key, mood (major/minor) and tempo pre-filled — so you can compose an original theme that fits alongside the track you loaded. Inspired by it, never copied from it.

Presets (the dropdown) are starting points that set the sliders — everything stays yours to reshape afterward. Combos to try: Speed down + Reverb up = dreamy; Bit crush + High cut = retro handheld; both filters toward the middle = old radio; Echo repeats high = dub tails.

Your remix presets (below the Lab, after your first save): tap a card to snap all sliders to those settings — even while music plays. ↗ Share posts the preset to the 🌐 Community board (a global gallery — a creator name is required and appears beside your posts); ⬇ JSON downloads it to back up or share as a file, and the Import JSON button on the Theme Forge page reads presets back in. ✕ deletes.

Loop is on by default so the track keeps going while you experiment — that's the fastest way to learn what every slider does.

Score Composer
🎼 How to use the Score Composer

Quick start: pick a note length and octave, tap piano keys to lay down a few notes, then press Play. That's the whole loop — everything below is refinement. There are 22 instruments to choose from, so try a few voices on the same tune.

Adding notes: pick a duration (the note buttons run whole → sixteenth; ♩ is a quarter note), pick an octave, then tap a piano key — the note appears on the staff and plays instantly. The ♯/♭ toggle decides how the black keys are spelled (C♯ vs D♭ — same sound, different notation). Every note shows its letter name underneath — chords stack their letters, lowest at the bottom — so the staff doubles as a learn-to-read-music aid.

Editing: tap any note on the staff to select it (it turns gold). While selected, piano keys replace its pitch, duration buttons replace its length, a rest icon turns it into silence, and ◀ ▶ move the selection. Tap Done (or empty staff space) to go back to adding notes at the end.

Tie — the easy way: a tie holds a note longer by joining it to another note of the same pitch. Just place a note and press ⌣ Tie — it automatically adds a matching note and connects them with a curved line, and they play as one sustained sound. Press ⌣ Tie again to extend the hold even further (a chain of ties). Pressing Tie on an already-tied note removes the tie. (If you happen to have already placed a matching note right after, Tie just connects to that one instead of adding a new one.)

Adjusting a tied note: the note Tie creates is a normal note and starts selected, so you can change its length right away with a duration button if you don't want it the same length. Keep the pitch the same and it stays a true tie (one sustained sound); if you change its pitch, it simply becomes two separate notes instead.

When do you actually need a tie? Only for lengths a single note can't show — like holding a note for 5 beats (a whole note tied to a quarter), or sustaining across a barline. If the length you want fits one note value (including dotted: dotted quarter = 1½ beats, dotted half = 3), just pick that note instead — it's cleaner.

Chords: turn on Chord, then tap extra piano keys — they stack onto the last note (or the selected one) instead of adding new notes. Tapping a key already in the chord removes it again. Up to five pitches per chord.

Rests (the icon row) insert silence — each icon is a rest length, from whole to sixteenth; the dot applies to them too. Use rests for breathing room. · dot makes the next note half again as long (a dotted quarter = 1½ beats). ↩ Undo steps back through your recent edits.

The length readout above the staff updates live — total time at the current tempo, measures, and note count — so you always know how long your piece runs. With a note selected, ▶ From here plays from that point instead of the top.

Time signature controls where the barlines fall (4/4 = four quarter-note beats per measure; 6/8 = six eighth-notes). Clef moves the pitch range: treble for melodies, bass for low parts.

Instrument changes who performs your notes — the same score sounds completely different on harp vs organ vs chip square, so try a few. Tempo is the speed in beats per minute; the length readout recalculates instantly when you change it, so you can compose to a target time ("I need a 30-second jingle").

Play performs the score with your chosen instrument, follows along on the staff with a green highlight, and turns the page for you automatically. Sheets hold four lines each — use ‹ Prev / Next › under the staff to flip between pages (on a computer, two pages sit side by side like an open book). Export WAV renders it to a real audio file, and 🖼 Save sheet downloads the notation itself as a PNG image — with your title on top — ready to print or share. Save keeps it in Your scores below — stored on this device.

Your scores cards show the instrument, note count and length. Tap one to load it back into the editor and keep working. ↗ Share posts the score to the 🌐 Community board (a global gallery — a creator name is required and appears beside your posts), where anyone can play, download or keep it. ⬇ JSON downloads it as a file, readable back via Import JSON on the Theme Forge page. ✕ deletes.

If something sounds wrong: tap the note, check its octave (a right melody in the wrong octave sounds off), or ↩ Undo your way back. There's no wrong way to use this — the fastest way to learn is to add five random notes and press Play.

Forge Sequencer
Multi-track piano roll — layer instruments, tap the grid to place notes, hear them together.
🎹 New to sequencing? Start here

What a sequencer is: instead of writing one melody on a staff, you stack several tracks — each one instrument — and arrange when every note plays on a grid. Play them together and you get layered music: a melody, a bassline, and drums all at once. That's how most modern game and pop music is built.

The grid (piano roll): each track has a grid. Up/down is pitch — every row is labelled on the left with its note name (C, C#, D…), and the gold C rows are your anchor points. Left/right is time, flowing from the song's start to its end. Bold vertical lines are bar divisions; lighter ones are beats.

Chords across instruments: to make several instruments play different notes at the same moment (say piano on C, strings on E, bass on G all together), place a note in the same time column on each of those tracks. Same column = same instant; the row picks each instrument's pitch. That's the core trick of arranging.

Placing notes: pick a length (the 1–8 buttons = how many grid steps), then tap an empty cell. The note appears and is selected (gold outline). Tapping is all you need — no dragging — so the grid always scrolls smoothly to reach later bars or higher/lower pitches.

Selecting & deleting: tap a note to select it (gold outline). Tap the selected note again to delete it. Tap empty space to deselect.

Changing a note's length: select the note, then either tap a length button (1–8) or use – shorter / + longer. The length applies to the selected note (or, with nothing selected, to the next note you place).

Note names & flats: every row is labelled on the left; gold rows are C's. Black-key rows show as sharps (C#, D#…) or flats (Db, Eb…) via the ♯/♭ button — same pitches, your preference.

Velocity (the slider up top) sets how loud the next notes you place will be — brighter blocks are louder. Great for accents and making a line feel human instead of robotic.

Tracks — each has a header: rename it, pick its instrument, drag its VOL slider to balance it against the others (this works live while the song plays — you'll hear the change instantly, no need to restart), M to mute, S to solo (hear it alone), ⧉ to duplicate, ✕ to delete. Tap a track to select it — new notes go to the selected track.

Drum tracks — the + Drum track button makes a special track whose rows are drums (kick, snare, hi-hat, clap, tom) instead of pitches. Tap the grid to place hits. A kick on beat 1, snare on beat 3, and steady hi-hats is a classic starting groove.

Playing it back: Play runs the whole song with a white playhead line sweeping across every track in time. Tempo is speed (BPM); Bars is how long the song is; Grid sets how finely you can place notes (2/beat = eighth notes, 4/beat = sixteenths); Zoom +/− widens the columns so tapping the exact spot is easier; Loop repeats; Metronome adds a click to play along to.

Recording your voice — the 🎤 Vocal track button adds a track you sing or play into. Press ● Record: the song starts and your mic records at the same time, so you perform in time with it. Recording stops automatically at the end of the song (or tap ■ Stop to end a take early), then press Play to hear it mixed with the music. Grant microphone access when the browser asks. Turn the metronome on first if you want a click to follow.

Lining up your vocal (important): recordings usually land a little late versus the beat — especially with Bluetooth earbuds/mics, which add noticeable delay. Each vocal track has a SYNC slider: drag it right (positive ms) if your voice sounds late, left if early, until it sits in the pocket. If your voice comes in too quiet under the instruments, raise the BOOST slider on the vocal track (it goes well beyond the instruments' own level). VOL and BOOST both adjust live during playback, so you can balance the vocal against the music while it plays. This is normal for all recording apps — wired or the phone's built-in mic needs less sync adjustment than Bluetooth.

Note: recorded audio is kept for playback and WAV export, but it can't be stored in a saved project file (only the notes are). To keep a vocal take, Export WAV — that bakes voice + instruments into one audio file.

Your first 2 minutes: on the piano track, tap four notes going up the grid on beats 1-2-3-4. Add a drum track, put a kick on every beat. Press Play. Congratulations — you're sequencing. Now add a bass track down low and a lead up high.

Saving: Save song keeps the whole multi-track project on this device; Export WAV mixes all tracks into one audio file; ⬇ JSON shares a project others can load. This is a companion to Musical Forge Studio — same 22 instruments and sound engine.
